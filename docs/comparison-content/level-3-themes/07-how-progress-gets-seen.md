# Theme 7 — How progress gets seen, and by whom

How RSHE, Welsh CfW, CASEL, and REAL each distribute the seeing of student progress — who looks, what they look at, and how they record what they see.

---

How progress gets seen is the most pervasive comparative thread across the corpus. The four frameworks differ in who does the seeing — teachers, parents, practitioners across nested settings, or students themselves — and in what they look at: content coverage, deepening competence over phases, abilities practised across settings, or patterns across observed occasions.

In RSHE, progress is the school's responsibility within a national content specification. The framework names what pupils should learn by end of primary and end of secondary [rshe-foreword §1, §3], with within-phase sequencing left to the school. Parents have a structurally-named role: schools should "engage with parents on the content of RSHE and be transparent with parents about all materials used in RSHE" [rshe-foreword §15], and parents may "request that their child be withdrawn from some or all of sex education delivered as part of statutory RSE" [rshe-foreword §16]. The seer of progress is the school; the parent is consulted, may opt out of sex-education content, and from three terms before age sixteen the pupil can opt back in [rshe-foreword §19]. RSHE itself does not specify an external assessor.

Welsh CfW positions progression as teacher-mediated professional judgement against descriptions of learning at each Progression Step, with phases not strictly year-grouped — each phase consolidates and strengthens the same competencies, applied in new and relevant contexts [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §11]. Schools are also expected to "provide increasing opportunities for learners to help direct their learning as they progress" [cfw-rse-statutory-principles §9]. The seer is the teacher, exercising judgement against the framework's descriptions, with the learner increasingly co-directing what gets seen.

CASEL positions four nested settings as the contexts in which competence is practised and seen: Classrooms where teachers integrate SEL, Schools where leadership coordinates implementation in a climate where "all students and adults feel respected, supported, and engaged" [casel-framework-definition §11], Families & Caregivers where authentic school-family partnerships "can build strong connections that reinforce students' social and emotional development" [casel-framework-definition §12], and Communities offering "safe and developmentally rich settings for learning and development" [casel-framework-definition §13]. The grade-band continuum supplies the descriptive standard against which competence is observed. The seers are practitioners across these four settings, with families and communities part of the framework rather than a backdrop.

REAL anchors progress in teacher observation across multiple contexts. At LT 1.1 Band B, the teacher notices the student "selecting a strategy from their practised repertoire before the trigger escalates, across more than one setting" [real-framework-overview §5]. At Band F, the teacher or mentor notices the student articulating their personal regulation system "in a Reflection 360 conversation or spontaneous reflection" [real-framework-overview §7]. At LT 7.2 Band D, the teacher notices the student "articulating a specific adjustment they have made to their thinking or behaviour and describing its effect across more than one domain, without prompting" [real-framework-overview §66]. The seer is the teacher (or mentor in Reflection 360); the student also articulates progress unprompted as evidence at the upper bands. At LT 5.2 Band F, the seeing extends past school: the teacher or mentor notices that "the student's post-school choices (path, community involvement, roles) trace specifically to prior sustained engagement" [real-framework-overview §56]. Parents are not given a structural role in this seeing.

Each framework's choice of seer carries through into what gets seen. RSHE pairs school-as-seer with content-coverage as object; the parent is structurally consulted. CfW pairs teacher-as-seer with deepening competence as object; the learner co-directs. CASEL pairs practitioners-across-settings with abilities-in-context as object; families and communities are part of the framework. REAL pairs teacher-and-mentor with patterns across observed occasions; the student articulates progress unprompted at the upper bands. Each choice has consequences for what evidence the framework can collect and what happens when seers disagree.
