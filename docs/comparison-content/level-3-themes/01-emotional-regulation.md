# Theme 1 — Emotional regulation

How RSHE, Welsh CfW, CASEL, and REAL each treat the territory that REAL holds across LTs 1.1, 1.2, 4.4, and 4.5.

---

Emotional regulation runs through every wellbeing curriculum we look at. In REAL it surfaces in four learning targets: noticing internal states and applying regulation strategies (LT 1.1), reading others' emotional states and repairing relationships after conflict (LT 1.2), understanding emotional and relational development (LT 4.4), and managing emotional activation in practice (LT 4.5). Each external framework treats this content too, with different premises and different specifications.

RSHE specifies the territory directly as terminal knowledge for the end of primary and the end of secondary. By the end of primary, pupils should learn "how to recognise feelings and use varied vocabulary to talk about their own and others' feelings" [rshe-pilot-extracts §2] and "how to judge whether what they are feeling and how they are behaving is appropriate and proportionate" [rshe-pilot-extracts §3]. By the end of secondary, pupils recognise that "worrying and feeling down are normal, can affect everyone at different times and are not in themselves a sign of a mental health condition" [rshe-pilot-extracts §12]. Help-seeking is named — "where and how to seek support" [rshe-pilot-extracts §4]. Within-phase sequencing is left to schools.

In Welsh CfW the same territory is framed as deepening competence within a What Matters statement: "How we process and respond to our experiences affects our mental health and emotional well-being" [cfw-what-matters §6]. The progression runs from PS1 ("I can notice and communicate how I am feeling" [cfw-pilot-extracts §2]) to PS5 ("I can use my self-awareness to appreciate the complexity of my emotions and apply strategies to self-regulate them in a healthy way and to connect with others" [cfw-pilot-extracts §5]). PS5 reaches a culture-of-talking move that places the regulating individual inside a wider communal practice [cfw-pilot-extracts §6].

CASEL names the territory as an ability practised across settings. Self-Management is "the abilities to manage one's emotions, thoughts, and behaviors effectively in different situations and to achieve goals and aspirations" [casel-framework-definition §6]. The grade-band continuum makes the developmental shape concrete: at Grade 4–5, students "recognize when they are having strong emotions and use different strategies to self-regulate and respond to difficult situations" [casel-pilot-extracts §1]; by Grade 9–10, they "examine available support systems and coping skills to identify those that can help process thoughts and manage emotions and stress" [casel-pilot-extracts §12]. The four nested settings — Classrooms, Schools, Families & Caregivers, Communities — make the practice context part of the framework rather than a backdrop to it [casel-framework-definition §10–§13].

REAL's emotional-regulation content is held across the four learning targets and recorded as a pattern of student action across contexts. LT 1.1 records a band as established when the teacher notices the student "selecting a strategy from their practised repertoire before the trigger escalates, across more than one setting" [real-framework-overview §5]. LT 1.2 carries the empathy thread, reaching at Band F a teacher-noticed move where the student adjusts their response to someone whose experience is being shaped by structural or systemic factors, in ways that address those conditions rather than only the individual person [real-framework-overview §26]. LT 4.4 builds an explanatory science layer at Bands D and above — dual-process emotion, the stress-response window, habit loops, the neurobiology of attraction, rejection and loss, and attachment theory examined for its empirical core and popularised periphery [real-framework-overview §14, §15, §16]. LT 4.5 anchors progress in genuinely activated moments: strategy use is recorded only when the teacher sees it appear in real challenge, with decreasing reliance on adult scaffolding over time [real-framework-overview §48, §49].

Each framework is internally coherent on its own terms. RSHE specifies end-of-phase content. CfW frames progression as deepening competence revisited at each step. CASEL frames the territory as abilities practised across nested settings. REAL records bands when the strategy shows up in genuinely activated moments across contexts, with an explanatory science layer at the upper bands. The differences are real and we don't try to settle them here.
