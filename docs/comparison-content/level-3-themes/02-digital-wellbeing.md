# Theme 2 — Digital wellbeing

How RSHE, Welsh CfW, CASEL, and REAL each treat the territory that REAL holds across LTs 6.2, 8.1, 8.2, and 8.3.

---

Digital wellbeing is the most recently-arrived of the cross-cutting threads in this corpus, and the one where the four frameworks differ most in scope. The territory itself is younger than the older curriculum-design conventions still shaping each framework. In REAL it surfaces across four learning targets: evaluating health information for trustworthiness and evidence quality (LT 6.2), verifying claims in digital content (LT 8.1), analysing how digital products shape attention and behaviour (LT 8.2), and maintaining digital boundaries and online assertiveness (LT 8.3). The external frameworks differ in how much of this territory they cover and how they frame what they cover.

RSHE addresses the territory across primary and secondary online-safety content. At primary, pupils should learn "how to critically evaluate their online relationships and sources of information, including awareness of the risks associated with people they have never met" [rshe-pilot-extracts §8]. The secondary online-safety section is broader, naming "respectful online interactions", the persistence of shared material, "addictive design" within online-gaming risks, "search-engine information selection and targeting", "data and consent rights", and reporting routes for online abuse and bullying [rshe-pilot-extracts §21]. The framing is safeguarding-and-evaluation; mechanism-level analysis of how digital products are engineered to capture attention isn't separately developed.

In Welsh CfW the digital territory appears in two places. Purpose 1's vision of an ambitious capable learner names that learners "use digital technologies creatively to communicate, find and analyse information" [cfw-four-purposes §4] — productive-use framing. WM3 builds critical evaluation in general decision-making terms: at PS4, "research, examine and evaluate a range of evidence to make considered and informed decisions" [cfw-pilot-extracts §10]; at PS5, "critically evaluate factors and implications, including risks, when making decisions individually and collectively" [cfw-pilot-extracts §13]. Within the Health and Well-being Area's What Matters statements, digital settings do not have a digital-specific subdivision; healthy-relationships and decision-making territory carry the related content where it appears.

CASEL addresses digital settings within Relationship Skills. At Grade 6–8, students "demonstrate positive communication skills, including interactions on social and digital media, and reflect on the potential impact on their reputation and relationships" [casel-pilot-extracts §24]. The framing is interpersonal — what the student says online and how it lands. Verification methods, evidence-hierarchy work, and persuasive-design analysis are not part of the five-competency content [casel-framework-definition §3, §4].

REAL holds digital wellbeing across four learning targets. LT 8.1 teaches a named verification process — "SIFT: Stop, Investigate the source, Find better coverage, Trace claims to the original context" — applied to any claim, alongside the principle that "Believe, share, and act are three different decisions about a claim" [real-framework-overview §21, §22]. LT 6.2 builds an evidence hierarchy from anecdote through controlled study to systematic reviews of randomised controlled trials [real-framework-overview §63], and at Band F traces how a guideline was derived from its evidence base [real-framework-overview §64]. LT 8.2 names persuasive-design features at Band C — infinite scroll, autoplay, variable reward — and at Band E covers the attention-economy business model and surveillance capitalism [real-framework-overview §68, §69]. LT 8.3 reaches Band F where the student treats their digital life as "an expression of their values — what they engage with, what they create, what they refuse" [real-framework-overview §73].

Each framework is internally coherent on its own terms. RSHE specifies safeguarding, evaluation, and reporting content directly. CfW combines productive-use framing with general critical-evaluation skills, without a digital-specific subdivision in Health and Well-being. CASEL routes digital interaction through Relationship Skills with an interpersonal focus. REAL builds the territory across four learning targets — verification methods, evidence hierarchies, persuasive-design mechanisms, and digital life as values-expression. The territory itself is recent, and the four frameworks have reached it from different starting points; readers can follow the citations and judge the shapes against what their school actually needs.
