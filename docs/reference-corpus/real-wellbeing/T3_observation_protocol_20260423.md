# T3 Authenticity Observation Protocol
## All six T3 dispositional LTs: 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 3.2, 7.2, 8.3
## 23 April 2026

Companion protocol document to `REAL_Wellbeing_KUD_v3_20260423.md` and the QA Step 8 panel review (`REAL_Wellbeing_QA_Steps_6_8_20260423.md`). Authored in response to the cross-cutting panel synthesis finding that the T3 indicator set across LTs 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 3.2, 7.2 and 8.3 concentrates observer-calibration burden around three repeating phrases — "authentic," "genuine," "without becoming performative" / "without making it a public performance" — which are the correct dispositional concerns but are, on their own, the least observable words in the framework. The protocol operationalises the shared observer-calibration surface across all six T3 LTs, so that what was previously six separate inferential burdens for each teacher becomes one discipline applied consistently.

**Who this protocol is for.** Any teacher or observer drawing on T3 observation indicators when forming an assessment claim about a student's band in LTs 1.1, 1.2, 1.3, 3.2, 7.2 or 8.3. It is also for colleagues co-observing the same cohort, for the purpose of calibrating against each other before band claims are reported. It is not for student view.

**What this protocol is not.** It is not a chart revision. None of the KUD v3 T3 indicators is rewritten by this document. It is not a substitute for the exemplar libraries that belong to specific LTs. It is not a floor under which any observer may operate; observers who have not read and calibrated against the exemplar libraries for the relevant LT should not be forming band claims.

---

## 1. What "authentic" means in this framework — and what it does not mean

The word *authentic* (and its neighbours *genuine*, *without becoming performative*, *without turning it into a public performance*) appears in multiple T3 indicators across the six LTs in this scope. The word carries a specific operational meaning in this framework, and it does not carry the everyday meaning.

**What it means, operationally.** An authenticity inference is a claim that a student is demonstrating a disposition, not performing one. The framework treats such a claim as supportable only from *cross-context consistency* and *longitudinal stability* — that is, from evidence that the student demonstrates the indicator across different settings, with different audiences, on different occasions, across a sustained observation window (for T3 LTs, typically across a term with the same observer, with triangulation across observers where the claim bears on band promotion).

**What it does not mean.** Authenticity inferences must never be drawn from a single occasion, and must never be drawn from the observer's intuitive read of whether the student "seemed sincere." A confident student can say the right words; a quieter student can say little; both reads are invisible judgement tracks that become sites of observer bias. A student who demonstrates an indicator on one occasion has provided *a data point*, not *evidence of the disposition*. The data point is worth recording as evidence-present; it is not worth recording as a band claim.

**Why the framework takes this position.** The QA Step 8 panel flagged "genuine reflection rather than surface performance," "authentic rather than performative," "identity-deep," and "without turning it into a public performance" as the least falsifiable language in the framework. The panel did not recommend removing these concepts (Claxton correctly argued that disposition *is* the category the framework is trying to measure, and the alternative of retreating to performance-only language would hollow out what the T3 LTs are for). The panel recommended instead that authenticity inferences be protected by an external discipline — the cross-context, longitudinal, multi-observer evidence requirement — rather than by the observer's internal judgement. This protocol is that discipline.

---

## 2. Minimum evidence standard for a band claim

A T3 band claim must meet all three of the following conditions. Any claim resting on fewer than these is not a band claim; it is provisional evidence.

1. **At least two independent observations of the relevant indicator across different contexts.** "Different contexts" means meaningfully different settings or audiences — for example: classroom discussion and small-group work; subject lesson and pastoral conversation; seminar and informal corridor exchange; formal reflection task and unprompted written output. Two observations of the same indicator in the same type of setting, with the same peers and the same teacher, do not meet this bar.
2. **Triangulation across at least two observers, where the claim bears on band promotion.** The second observer need not be another teacher; it may be the student themselves (structured self-reflection), a parent or caregiver (if they have noticed the move at home), or Strive data where the relevant LT has a Strive signal. The triangulation requirement does not apply to routine evidence-logging — a single teacher may note evidence-present — but it applies when the evidence is being mobilised to move a student from one band to the next.
3. **At least one counterexample observation per unit of work.** A counterexample is an observation that surprised the observer or contradicted their prior view of the student. The requirement is not that the student must disconfirm the indicator; it is that the observer must have actively looked for and recorded an observation that would have surprised them. If no such observation exists on the record, the observer is at risk of confirming a prior view rather than observing a current student. The counterexample observation is recorded even when it is consistent with the existing band claim — it is the act of looking for it that protects the claim, not the outcome of the looking.

These three conditions apply across all six T3 LTs in the scope of this protocol. They do not replace the band-specific indicators; they are the evidential floor under which a band claim is not supportable regardless of how strong any single indicator reading appears.

## 3. Handling conflicting observer reports

When two observers disagree on whether an indicator has been met — or on which band the student is in — the disagreement is not noise to be averaged out. It is developmental information about the student, the indicator, or both.

**Do not resolve disagreement by defaulting to the teacher's view.** The teacher is one observer among multiple. A parent reporting that a student shows a pattern at home that the teacher does not see in class is reporting real data, even if the teacher does not trust their own reading of it. A student's self-report that they do not feel the thing the teacher is observing is data about the student's self-concept and should be surfaced rather than overridden.

**Do record the disagreement.** The observation log for the relevant LT should note: which observers observed; what each observer saw or did not see; in what context; on what date. The disagreement is not a flaw in the assessment process; it is a finding about the student.

**Do surface the disagreement in the developmental conversation with the student.** A conversation that begins "your swimming coach sees you doing this, but I rarely see it in my class — what's the difference for you?" is pedagogically stronger than a conversation that begins "here is your band." The first treats the student as someone whose own account bears on the claim; the second treats the student as the object of the claim.

**Do not hide disagreement behind a consensus band.** If a band claim is being made despite unresolved observer disagreement, the claim must be recorded as contested, with the specific points of disagreement named. Collapsing contested evidence into a clean band claim is the failure mode this protocol is designed to prevent.

## 4. Partial evidence at Band F across all six T3 LTs

Band F indicators across the T3 LTs in scope typically describe complex, multi-condition observables. Examples:

- LT 1.1 Band F — an articulation move combined with cross-context consistency.
- LT 1.2 Band F — cross-context consistency, addressing conditions rather than only the individual, and absence of performative recognition.
- LT 1.3 Band F — engagement with challenge, absence of dismissal, absence of retreat, absence of projection onto peers (four observations in a single indicator).
- LT 3.2 Band F — resilience across multiple stressor types, with system-level self-care framing, over a sustained period.
- LT 7.2 Band F — cross-context consistency of self-direction with identity-level framing.
- LT 8.3 Band F — cross-context agency in digital environments, holding regardless of who is watching, without turning it into a public performance.

In each case, the Band F indicator stacks several conditions. Partial evidence — a student who meets some conditions but not others — is common in the Band F observation window and should not be forced into a met/not-met binary.

**Recording partial evidence.** When an observer sees a Band F indicator met on some conditions but not others, the record should name which specific conditions were met and which were not met on that occasion. Absence of a condition is not the same as evidence of its opposite: it is "not yet seen," which is different from "seen and failed." The distinction matters for feedback to the student and for subsequent observation planning (the observer returns to watch specifically for what was not yet seen, rather than starting the observation over).

**When partial evidence is stronger than full evidence.** Partial evidence observed across two or more occasions, showing progressive consolidation over time, is stronger evidence of Band F than full evidence on a single occasion. A student who meets two of the four LT 1.3 Band F conditions in October, three in January, and all four in April is producing the kind of longitudinal evidence the Band F indicator is designed to measure. A student who meets all four on one afternoon in March, never observed again, is producing a performance data point — worth noting, not sufficient for a Band F claim.

**When partial evidence does not count toward a Band F claim.** Partial evidence that is not consolidating — conditions met inconsistently, no progressive trend, some conditions never observed — supports a description of the student as *approaching Band F* or *demonstrating Band F behaviours inconsistently*. Both descriptions are accurate and useful; neither is a Band F claim.

This partial-evidence recording discipline replaces, for the purposes of this framework, any approach that would consolidate multi-condition Band F indicators into simpler two-condition indicators in order to ease observer load. The case for that alternative approach (specifically Christodoulou's LT 1.3 Band F two-observations-plus-two-conditions-present recommendation) is addressed in section 5 below.

## 5. LT 1.3 Band F — Christodoulou dissent recorded

**The recommendation.** In QA Step 8 panel review, Christodoulou recommended that the LT 1.3 Band F indicator, which currently stacks four observations (engagement with challenge; absence of dismissal; absence of retreat; absence of projection onto peers), be consolidated into two positive indicators with the remaining two logged as conditions-present rather than as positive indicators. Her reasoning: assessment protocols that ask observers to make four simultaneous observations on a single indicator tend in practice to reduce to whichever observation the observer finds easiest, which silently collapses the four-observation stack into a one-observation proxy.

**The decision not to adopt the consolidation.** The decision taken in this framework is not to adopt Christodoulou's recommendation. The reasoning is recorded here so that the dissent does not become invisible:

- Band F is a T3 dispositional indicator observed over a longitudinal window. In that context, the partial-evidence recording protocol (section 4 of this document, and the more detailed protocol specific to LT 1.3 Band F in `LT_1_3_observation_exemplar_library_20260423.md`) is the correct instrument. It preserves the four-observation standard, and it surfaces partial evidence as partial rather than forcing the observer into a met/not-met binary.
- Consolidating to two positive indicators would weaken the Band F standard without resolving the observer-calibration problem the recommendation was designed to address. A two-indicator Band F can also collapse to whichever of the two an observer finds easiest; the fix for observer-calibration is not fewer conditions, it is better calibration against the conditions that are there.
- Christodoulou's specific concern — that observers will collapse four-observation stacks to the easiest of the four — is addressed by the LT 1.3 exemplar library, which provides anchor cases (two "met" anchors, one false-positive anchor, one false-negative anchor) at Band F. The exemplars make explicit what each clause looks like when met, when missing, and when surface-similar-but-not-met. The exemplar library is the right instrument for the observer-calibration problem; indicator consolidation is the wrong instrument.

**Where the dissent is recorded.** This section (section 5 of the T3 observation protocol) is the canonical location of the Christodoulou dissent on the LT 1.3 Band F indicator structure. The KUD v3 chart preserves the four-observation wording from v2; the authoring notes on the KUD v3 LT 1.3 entry reference the partial-evidence protocol and the exemplar library but do not themselves record the dissent.

## 6. Per-LT observer-calibration notes

For each of the six T3 LTs, this section names the specific bands where the QA Step 8 panel review identified observer-calibration risk, with a one-sentence calibration note per band. These notes are summaries; the full calibration instrument for each band is the relevant exemplar library (existing or to be authored), not this section.

### LT 1.1 — Self-Awareness & Regulation

- **Band F — articulation-as-evidence risk.** Band F asks the student to articulate their self-regulation system; the observer-calibration risk is that articulate students who perform the articulation well can score Band F without the underlying cross-context behavioural consistency. The Reflection 360 triangulation requirement is the mitigation; articulation evidence alone, without cross-context behavioural evidence, is not sufficient for a Band F claim.

### LT 1.2 — Social Awareness & Empathy

- **Band F — "addressed conditions rather than only the individual" distinction.** Band F asks the student to respond to a peer's distress in a way that addresses the conditions producing it rather than only the individual experiencing it. The observer must be calibrated on this distinction before scoring. A calibration note follows this section as a named sub-section ("LT 1.2 Band F calibration note — addressed conditions vs addressed individual").

#### LT 1.2 Band F calibration note — addressed conditions vs addressed individual

**What the indicator asks.** Band F indicator (paraphrased): the teacher notices the student, across two or more occasions in different contexts, responding to a peer's distress in a way that addresses the conditions producing the distress (environmental, social, systemic, structural) rather than only the individual person's feeling — and does so without performing the recognition for an audience.

**Why the distinction matters.** At earlier bands (B, C, D) empathic response means noticing the individual's feeling and responding to it. The Band F move is different: the student perceives that an individual's distress is produced by something beyond the individual (a condition that is currently producing this distress for this person and will continue producing it for others unless addressed), and their response addresses that condition. The earlier-band move is not wrong at Band F — it is insufficient. The Band F claim requires the condition-addressing move in addition to, not instead of, the individual-addressing one.

**One concrete example that meets the indicator.** A student in a Year 10 tutor group notices that a classmate is visibly distressed after a lesson in which a teacher used a worked example involving immigration that the classmate (whose family is immigrant-background) clearly found reductive. The student checks in with the classmate privately first ("are you OK?") and the classmate confirms what happened. The student then, without being asked, writes a private message to the teacher that evening saying: *"I don't know if you noticed, but the immigration example in today's lesson landed hard on [named classmate]. I think it's because it used 'immigrants' as a group the way the lesson treated 'variables' — as interchangeable. I'm telling you not to get [classmate] in trouble and not because I want an apology — I'm telling you because the next lesson will have another student it lands on. Would it help if I suggested a different example?"* The student is addressing the condition (the teacher's framing, which is reproducible across lessons and across students), not only the individual (whose distress has been responded to privately and whose consent to escalate has been sought). This is the Band F move.

**One concrete example that does not meet the indicator despite surface similarity.** A student in the same tutor group notices the same classmate's distress and responds: *"That must have been hard. It wasn't fair. Do you want to talk to Mr X together? I'll come with you."* The response is empathic, appropriate, and in the right direction. It addresses the individual's feeling; it offers support for escalation; it names the unfairness. But it does not address the *condition* — the pedagogical framing, reproducible across lessons — in the student's own response. This meets Band E (attuned empathic response with agency); it does not meet Band F. The observer who scores this as Band F has collapsed the Band F move ("addressed the condition producing the distress") into the Band E move ("attuned to the individual experiencing the distress"). The two are related; they are not the same.

**Calibration discipline.** When scoring Band F for LT 1.2, the observer asks: did the student's response address something that, if left unaddressed, would continue producing distress for others in the future? If the response terminates at the individual ("I am here for you; let's go together; I will support you"), it is Band E. If the response addresses the upstream condition ("this framing / policy / pattern / environment will continue to produce distress; here is a specific way of altering it"), it is Band F. The presence of the earlier-band move does not disqualify a Band F claim; the absence of the condition-addressing move does.

### LT 1.3 — Personal Identity & Cultural Self-Awareness

- **Band D.** Previously a high-calibration-risk band due to the v2 "genuine reflection rather than surface performance" wording; now addressed by the Christodoulou behavioural-anchor replacement (KUD v3) and the LT 1.3 exemplar library. Observers working at Band D for LT 1.3 read the exemplar library before observing.
- **Band F.** Four-observation stacking (engagement; no dismissal; no retreat; no projection onto peers) is addressed by the partial-evidence protocol in section 4 of this document and in the LT 1.3 exemplar library; the Christodoulou consolidation recommendation is not adopted (see section 5 above).

### LT 3.2 — Self-Care & Resilience

- **Band D — AND-conjoined indicator.** Band D Do indicator is AND-conjoined (sustained self-care across multiple stressor types AND peer non-directive support). The observer-calibration risk is that the two conjuncts are different kinds of behavioural evidence (the first is longitudinal self-care observation; the second is peer-interaction observation) and tend to be logged in different observation settings. Observers must triangulate across settings, not treat one conjunct as proxy for the other.
- **Band E — "authentic rather than performative."** The language "authentic rather than performative" appears in the Band E indicator. The operational meaning per section 1 of this protocol applies: authenticity inferences drawn from cross-context consistency and longitudinal stability, never from a single occasion; no observer inference from "seemed sincere."

### LT 7.2 — Self-Direction in Practice

- **Band F — "identity-level disposition" inferential gap.** Band F asks for a claim that the student's self-direction is identity-deep — that is, that it holds across domains, contexts, and roles to a degree that warrants calling it a settled feature of who the student is as a learner and person. The inferential gap is that cross-context consistency (which the observer can see) does not by itself warrant the identity-level inference (which is a stronger claim). Observers must be calibrated on what cross-context consistency looks like when it does and does not warrant the identity-level claim. Exemplar library appended to this document (Appendix A).

### LT 8.3 — Digital Assertiveness & Wellbeing Strategies

- **Band F — "without turning it into a public performance."** Band F asks the student to exercise cross-context agency in digital environments in a way that holds regardless of who is watching, and specifically does not turn the agency into a performance for an audience. The observer-calibration risk is that digitally-fluent students can produce content *about* their agency that looks indistinguishable from the agency itself; the indicator requires the distinction. Exemplar library appended to this document (Appendix B).

## 7. Cross-cutting summary

- Authenticity inferences are drawn from cross-context consistency and longitudinal stability, never from a single occasion.
- A band claim requires at least two independent observations across different contexts; triangulation across at least two observers when the claim bears on band promotion; at least one counterexample observation per unit of work.
- Observer disagreement is recorded as data and surfaced in developmental conversations. It is not resolved by defaulting to the teacher's view.
- At Band F, partial evidence is recorded specifically — which conditions met, which not yet seen — and partial evidence consolidating across occasions is stronger evidence than full evidence on a single occasion.
- The LT 1.3 Band F four-observation indicator structure is preserved. Christodoulou dissent recorded in section 5. Observer-calibration handled by the exemplar library, not by indicator consolidation.
- Band-level calibration concerns across the six T3 LTs are listed in section 6; full calibration instruments live in the per-LT exemplar libraries (LT 1.3 Bands D and F already authored; LT 7.2 Band F and LT 8.3 Band F appended to this document as Appendix A and Appendix B).

# Appendix A — LT 7.2 Band F observation-indicator exemplar library

**Scope.** LT 7.2 Band F only. Bands A–E are not covered here; if an exemplar library is later extended to cover them, it should live as an addendum to this appendix rather than as a replacement.

**Indicator (paraphrased from KUD v3 LT 7.2 Band F Do).** The teacher notices the student, across multiple contexts and a sustained observation window, consistently directing their own thinking and learning — noticing, analysing, and intentionally adjusting their own patterns without external prompting — to a degree that warrants calling self-direction a settled feature of who the student is as a learner and person, not only a capacity they can perform when the context invites it.

**Calibration concern.** The inferential gap at Band F is between cross-context consistency of the observable behaviours (which the observer can see) and the identity-level claim (which is a stronger claim than the observable data by itself supports). A student who performs self-direction consistently across a term may still be performing it in response to a shared contextual feature (a supportive school culture, a strong peer group, a particular teacher's expectation). The Band F claim is that the student would continue to demonstrate the behaviour in contexts lacking those features — which the observer cannot directly see, but which can be inferred from specific kinds of evidence.

### Two anchors — "indicator is met"

**A-MET-1.** A Year 13 student has, across the observed term, applied the structured metacognitive protocol she learned in Year 12 to three different domains: her A-level coursework, her role coaching a Year 8 peer group in debate, and a volunteer project at a community centre outside school. In each domain the observer sees the same pattern: she names a pattern, identifies sustaining conditions, designs an intervention, tracks its effects, iterates. The observer has also heard the student, in two separate Reflection 360 conversations across the term, describe the same pattern of thinking about domains she has not yet entered — a summer internship she is about to begin, a first university term she is planning for. The student talks about these future contexts not as places where she will "have to" do the protocol, but as places where she expects to carry it because it is how she now learns. A parent meeting records the same observation from a different context: she has started applying the same analytical habit to conflicts within the family ("mapping what sustains the pattern rather than reacting to the surface of the argument"). The observer has cross-context consistency across three current domains, explicit anticipation of the practice holding in future contexts the observer is not party to, and triangulation from a parent context. The identity-level claim — that self-direction is a settled feature of this student, not a performance she produces under school conditions — is warranted.

**A-MET-2.** A student who is neurodivergent and who, at earlier bands, had often presented self-direction as "compliance with the strategy the school wants me to use" has, across Year 12, visibly shifted. She has adapted three elements of the school's structured metacognitive protocol to work with how her attention and executive function actually operate — ditching the written tracking log (which her observer knows she has said in her SENCo conversations felt "like a second layer of failure") and replacing it with a voice-memo format she reviews weekly. Across two terms the observer has seen her apply this personalised version across her academic subjects, a personal project on textile design, and her role in the school's neurodivergent peer-support group (where she has shared the adaptation with three other students, each of whom has taken pieces of it for themselves). In her final Reflection 360 conversation she says: *"I used to do this because the school wanted me to. Now I do it because it's how I figure out what I think. If I went somewhere that didn't ask for it, I'd still do it — I might just stop calling it that."* Cross-context consistency is evidenced within the school. The explicit statement about contexts that "don't ask for it" is not itself proof — students can rehearse such statements — but the adaptation history (the specific rejection of the school's default format in favour of one that suits her, sustained across two terms) is the evidence that makes the identity-level claim warranted. She is not performing the school's script; she has authored her own.

### One anchor — "indicator is NOT met despite surface similarity"

**A-NOT-MET-1.** A Year 13 student who presents as articulate, high-achieving, and visibly self-directed across the term produces consistently strong Reflection 360 content and consistently applies the structured metacognitive protocol in the formats the school has taught. Cross-context consistency is present: the protocol appears in his A-level coursework, in his extracurricular work as a debate team captain, and in his leadership of a tutoring scheme. His articulation about self-direction is fluent; he can describe the protocol, name its phases, and explain why it matters. The observer, reviewing the evidence at the end of the term, is tempted to score Band F. But the counterexample-observation discipline (section 2 of this protocol) has surfaced a pattern: in two separate contexts that fell outside the school's default format — a summer work placement on which he debriefed with the observer, and a family context he mentioned in passing — he describes not using the protocol at all, and describes the thinking that produced those decisions as "just what you do." The observable school behaviours are consistent; the cross-context inference is that his self-direction tracks the *format* the school provides, not the underlying disposition. The indicator is not met. A Band E claim, with a developmental conversation about whether the protocol is a format to perform or a mechanism to internalise, is the correct response.

*Calibration note — false positive.* Fluency, articulation, and cross-context consistency within the school's default formats are not sufficient for Band F. The identity-level claim requires evidence that the disposition holds in contexts where the format is not provided — either through observed application in non-school contexts, or through the kind of personal-adaptation history that evidences authorship of the practice rather than performance of it. When in doubt, the observer defers the Band F claim and continues observing into the next year.

### One anchor — "indicator is MET in an unexpected form"

**A-MET-UNEXPECTED-1.** A Year 13 student who has a long pattern of low engagement with the school's structured metacognitive protocol ("I don't do written reflections, they don't work for me") and whose Reflection 360 evidence across earlier bands has been sparse, has across the final year produced a different kind of evidence. He runs a small online business he started before joining the school; across the year his business has gone through two operational changes, and in both cases the observer has seen — through conversations the student has voluntarily initiated, and through a set of planning documents he has shared without being asked — the student articulate the problem, identify what was sustaining it, design a change, track its effects over six to eight weeks, and iterate. The pattern matches the structured metacognitive protocol precisely; the student has never called it that. In a final conversation the observer asks whether this is the same thing as the school's protocol. The student says: *"I guess it is. I just didn't know that's what you were trying to teach me."* The observer does not have the school's standard evidence format (written reflections, Reflection 360 transcripts in the school's template). The observer has richer evidence in a different format (the business context, the planning documents, the observed iteration) that meets the Band F claim more fully than the standard format could have. The identity-level claim is warranted.

*Calibration note — false negative.* Students whose cross-context self-direction operates outside the school's default formats can evidence Band F more fully than students whose evidence is entirely within those formats. The observer's job is to recognise the move, not the packaging. A student who does the thinking in a business context, in an activist context, in a creative or technical domain, or in their family — and who does it without the school's prompts — may be producing stronger Band F evidence than a student whose Reflection 360 documents are polished. The exemplar library, not the evidence format, is the calibration instrument.

# Appendix B — LT 8.3 Band F observation-indicator exemplar library

**Scope.** LT 8.3 Band F only. Bands A–E are not covered here; if an exemplar library is later extended to cover them, it should live as an addendum to this appendix.

**Indicator (paraphrased from KUD v3 LT 8.3 Band F Do).** The teacher notices the student, across multiple digital contexts and a sustained observation window, exercising agency inside digital environments — in how they engage, disengage, post, refrain from posting, respond to others, protect their attention, manage their wellbeing — in a way that holds regardless of who is watching, and specifically without turning the agency itself into a public performance for an audience.

**Calibration concern.** Digital environments are performance environments by default. A student who is genuinely cross-context agentic in digital spaces is doing something that, from the outside, can look indistinguishable from a student who is producing public content about their agency. Both can show up as consistent, visible, articulate. The Band F move requires the observer to distinguish between the agency itself (which is often invisible) and the representation of the agency (which is often visible and is not the same thing).

### Two anchors — "indicator is met"

**B-MET-1.** A Year 12 student has, across the term, made several visible changes to their digital practice: removed social media applications from their phone for a defined period during exam season; set a no-phone rule at family dinners; started a group chat with three friends where the agreement is that no one posts screenshots to public channels. The observer has also noticed what the student has *not* done across the same period: she has not posted about any of the changes; she has not made her digital-minimalism a visible feature of her social-media presence; she has not given her friends permission to cite her as an example. When another student in a tutor group session asks her publicly about her phone habits (knowing she has been thinking about them), she answers briefly, factually, and without self-promotion: *"I took the apps off for exam season. It helped. I'll probably put some back after. That's about it."* She does not turn the moment into a longer reflection for the group. Cross-context evidence: the observer has seen similar self-direction in the student's digital practice in a different setting — she has voluntarily moderated her participation in a group chat that was becoming hostile to a peer, and has done so through private messages rather than public ones, again leaving no visible record of the moderation for others to admire. The indicator is met: the agency is consistent, cross-context, and specifically not performed.

**B-MET-2.** A Year 13 student who is active on two public platforms (a creative-work Instagram; a writing account on another platform) has, across the year, made a series of decisions the observer has come to understand through Reflection 360 conversations and through quiet unprompted comments: he has stopped checking the platforms more than twice a week, has turned off every notification except direct messages from a small list of people, has deleted a dormant third account that he said was "producing things I didn't mean for anyone." None of these decisions has been posted about on either of his active platforms; the platforms themselves show no change in tone or content that could be read as a public performance of digital discipline. In parallel, the student has continued to post creative work at roughly the same rate, with the same quality and register as before. In a conversation the observer asks how he reconciles staying active with cutting his checking. He says: *"I post what I want to post. Whether anyone's seeing it isn't really the point of posting. That's what I had to figure out."* Cross-context evidence: the same pattern holds in his participation in a Discord community outside school (which the observer knows about from a conversation with him and which is corroborated by a sibling in a parent meeting) — he is active, contributes, and does not seek visibility within it. The Band F move is the separation between posting-as-expression and posting-for-visibility; the student has made it, and has not turned making it into its own public position.

### One anchor — "indicator is NOT met despite surface similarity"

**B-NOT-MET-1.** A Year 12 student is visibly thoughtful about digital life. Across the term she has posted, on her public social media: a photograph of a stack of books with the caption *"no phone days are the best days"*; a short video essay on the attention economy that closes with a call for others to think about their screen time; a reflection on a book she read about smartphones in adolescence. She has spoken in a school assembly about her digital wellbeing choices. Her posts are articulate, visible, consistently framed around digital-minimalism values, and admired by peers. The observer is initially inclined to score Band F: the student is clearly thinking about digital environments, clearly exercising choices, clearly consistent. But the counterexample-observation discipline has surfaced a pattern the public content does not: the student's actual phone-use data (which she has voluntarily shared with the observer in a Reflection 360 conversation) has not changed across the term; the posts about no-phone days are posted on the no-phone days; she has told the observer she checks the platforms "more than I'd like" in order to see how the posts are performing. The performance is consistent across contexts. The underlying agency is not. The indicator is not met — the student is producing public content *about* agency rather than exercising the agency the indicator asks about.

*Calibration note — false positive.* Public content about digital wellbeing is easily mistaken for evidence of digital wellbeing. The observer's question is not whether the content exists but whether the behaviour the content claims to represent exists when no content is being produced. When the performance and the behaviour are the same act, the behaviour is the performance, and the indicator is not met. Band F requires agency that holds specifically when no performance is available.

### One anchor — "indicator is MET in an unexpected form"

**B-MET-UNEXPECTED-1.** A Year 13 student presents as digitally-disengaged in visible school settings: rarely talks about platforms, has no public social-media profile the observer is aware of, rarely mentions digital life in Reflection 360 conversations. On the surface, the observer would not expect this LT to yield Band F evidence — there is nothing visible to score. But across the year, several specific observations accumulate: the student has quietly built and maintained a small encrypted group chat for six peers who had been targeted by homophobic content on a public platform, has moderated it with explicit agreements about what is and is not shared outside it, and has supported three of those peers through individual incidents without ever naming what she did in any public or semi-public school setting. A school pastoral lead knows about this (a peer mentioned it); a parent knows (the student's mother mentioned it, unprompted, at a parents' evening). The observer herself only learns about it late in the year, through a conversation in which the student is deliberately not performing the role, because a younger student is going through something similar and the older student is trying to work out how best to help without replicating her own pattern of not asking for support. Cross-context evidence: the same pattern — agency in digital environments, exercised without audience, specifically not turned into public recognition — holds in the encrypted chat, in the one-on-one peer support, in the present conversation with the observer. The indicator is met. The evidence is rich, sustained, and specifically non-performative; it exists precisely because the student has refused to perform it.

*Calibration note — false negative.* A student whose Band F digital agency is genuinely non-performative will, by definition, produce less evidence in the channels observers usually watch. The Band F claim requires the observer to look in channels that do not perform — private conversations, corroborations from other adults who know the student, incidents the student has handled without representation — and to take seriously the absence of public representation as a signal rather than as a gap in the evidence. A student who does the thing without posting about it, who helps without publicising it, who disengages without announcing it, may be producing the strongest Band F evidence in the framework.
