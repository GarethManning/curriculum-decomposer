# LT 1.3 Observation-Indicator Exemplar Library
## Bands D and F
## 23 April 2026

Companion artefact to `REAL_Wellbeing_KUD_v3_20260423.md`, authored in response to the QA Step 8 panel consensus recommendation on LT 1.3 (mean 87.8, flagged for observer-calibration work at Band D and Band F). Bands A–C and E are not included in this library because they did not carry the observer-calibration burden the panel flagged; if an exemplar library is later extended to cover them, it should live as an addendum to this file rather than as a replacement.

**Who this is for.** Teachers observing LT 1.3 in real school contexts (not simulations), and their colleagues reading through the indicators to calibrate against each other before co-observing. The library is not for student view. The exemplars are illustrative reference cases, not scripts — a real observation that looks *like* an exemplar meets the indicator; an observation that looks *like* a surface-similar case does not.

**What counts as "observation."** For LT 1.3, observation is not a single-occasion snapshot. The indicator at each band is met on the basis of what the teacher sees a student say or do across the relevant observation window (for T3 LTs, typically across a term with the same observer, with triangulation across observers where the claim bears on band promotion). Single-occasion evidence is not sufficient to claim the indicator is met; single-occasion evidence is sufficient to flag the indicator as "evidence present — return to watch."

**Guardrail.** None of the exemplars below is a script for teaching. Using them as prompts ("tell me something like this") would convert the indicator into a performance test, which is what the v3 wording fix was designed to prevent. The exemplars are for observer calibration only.

## Band D — behavioural anchor indicator (v3)

> "The teacher notices the student, in classroom discussion or a reflective written task, naming a specific unchosen group membership they hold (for example: the family they were born into, the first language they grew up with, the nationality on their passport, or how others read their body or appearance) and giving a specific example of how that membership has shaped one of their own experiences — where the example is not a repetition of a class discussion example offered by a peer or teacher."

### Two anchors — "indicator is met"

**D-MET-1.** In a history lesson on migration in the twentieth century, the teacher opens a discussion on family language background. A student, unprompted, says: *"My grandmother speaks Hungarian and my mum only speaks English to me, and I realised last year that I was kind of embarrassed to use Hungarian words in front of my friends at school — it wasn't something I chose, it was just what happened at home, but I noticed it was shaping how I behaved with my friends."* The student has named an unchosen membership (first language at home), given a specific example from their own life (embarrassment about using Hungarian words in front of English-speaking friends), and the example is not lifted from the class's earlier shared material.

**D-MET-2.** In a written reflection responding to an open prompt ("describe something about yourself that shapes how you see the world"), a student writes: *"I am the youngest of four kids in a family where my older siblings were all boys. I didn't pick being the youngest and I didn't pick being the only girl. Last week at swimming I noticed I was automatically waiting for someone older to go first on the diving board — I think that's a pattern from being youngest in my family, not a pattern that makes sense at swim club where age doesn't matter like that."* Unchosen membership is named (birth-order position; and gender position within sibling group); the example is from the student's own life, not repeated from class material; the experience-shaping link is made specifically.

### One anchor — "indicator is NOT met despite surface similarity"

**D-NOT-MET-1.** In the same class discussion as D-MET-1, another student says: *"Some people speak different languages at home and school, and that's because language is part of culture, and culture shapes how you see things."* This is a fluent, correct sentence, but it names no unchosen membership the student themselves holds and gives no example from their own life — it is a summary of the Band C content restated. A student can score strongly on Band C's "pauses to consider how their own background might be influencing their reaction" using precisely this kind of fluency. The Band D indicator asks for something different: a specific unchosen membership the student holds, plus a specific shaping example from their own life.

*Calibration note — false positive.* Fluency about culture, identity, or "how people's backgrounds shape them" does not meet Band D. The Band D move is that the student produces evidence *about themselves specifically* that is not summary or restatement.

### One anchor — "indicator is MET in an unexpected form"

**D-MET-UNEXPECTED-1.** A student who rarely speaks in whole-class discussion writes in the margin of a handed-in personal essay, separately from the essay's main body: *"My mum moved here from Vietnam when she was nineteen and I look Vietnamese to the teachers at school. Something I noticed this year is that I get asked about 'my culture' more than my friends whose families have always been here — and that isn't something about what I chose, it's something about how I look."* The student has named an unchosen membership (how others read their appearance), given a specific example from their own experience (being asked about culture more than peers whose families are not immigrant-background), and the example is not a repetition of class discussion material. The evidence is in a margin note rather than the main reflection, and the student has not performed the move in class — but the indicator is met.

*Calibration note — false negative.* Absence from class discussion is not absence of the indicator. Students who find identity talk in whole-class settings exposing may evidence the indicator in writing, in 1:1 conversation with a teacher, in a reflective journal, or in a family or mentor conversation reported to the observer. The observer looks for the move, not the setting.

## Band F — four-observation indicator (v2 wording preserved in v3)

> "The teacher notices the student engaging with perspectives that challenge their own identity framework — including uncomfortable ones — without dismissing them or retreating to a fixed position, and without projecting their own resolution process onto peers navigating different questions."

This indicator stacks four observations: (1) engagement with challenge, (2) absence of dismissal, (3) absence of retreat to a fixed position, (4) absence of projection onto peers. The panel flagged the four-observation stacking as an observer-calibration burden; a note on how to handle partial evidence follows the exemplars.

### Two anchors — "indicator is met"

**F-MET-1.** A Year 11 student who identifies strongly with their Hungarian Catholic background is in a seminar on contemporary religion-and-ethics questions. A classmate (raised in a secular family) presents a position that directly challenges a view the student holds, on a question where the student feels strongly. Across the seminar the teacher observes: the student asks the classmate a specific clarifying question about the reasoning rather than a rhetorical one (engagement); the student names where they disagree without dismissing the classmate's underlying concern (no dismissal); the student says *"I'm still holding my view but I'm less sure about part of it now"* (no retreat to a fixed position); when a third student later presents a view in a completely different direction, the first student does not say to the classmate *"well, now you should think what I had to think"* (no projection). The four observations occur across the same seminar and a subsequent one-to-one follow-up in the corridor.

**F-MET-2.** In a Reflection 360 conversation, a student describes reading a book whose protagonist lives a life organised by a value system the student does not share. The student says: *"It was uncomfortable — the book kept making arguments I couldn't immediately find what was wrong with. I noticed I wanted to stop reading it, and I also noticed that wanting to stop wasn't a good reason. I'm not sure what I think about the main argument now. I asked [a named friend] what she thought because her background is different from mine and I wanted to hear a perspective that wasn't mine. I didn't tell her what I decided — she's working it out differently, and that's her process."* Engagement present; absence of dismissal evidenced ("couldn't find what was wrong"); absence of retreat evidenced ("I'm not sure what I think now" — held, not closed); absence of projection evidenced (explicit statement that the friend's process is hers, separate).

### One anchor — "indicator is NOT met despite surface similarity"

**F-NOT-MET-1.** A student in the same seminar as F-MET-1 engages fluently with a challenging view: asks the right kinds of questions, names the position clearly, and is articulate about the different value systems in play. But as the seminar ends, they turn to the classmate who presented the challenge and say: *"I had to work through this too at your age, and honestly when you think about it more, you'll see why my position holds up."* The first three observations (engagement, no dismissal, no retreat) are met; the fourth (no projection) is not. The student is imposing their own resolution process — "when you think about it more, you'll see why my position holds up" — on a peer navigating a different question. This is the failure mode the indicator's fourth clause was written to catch.

*Calibration note — false positive.* Articulate, calm, intellectually engaged responses can mask projection. Projection is usually in the small moves — "you'll see," "when you're ready," "I went through this too" — where the student frames the peer's process as one that should land where the student's has. If the engagement looks adult-level, check especially for the fourth observation.

### One anchor — "indicator is MET in an unexpected form"

**F-MET-UNEXPECTED-1.** A student is in an informal conversation with a younger peer who is working out a question about their own cultural or religious identity. The younger peer asks directly: *"How did you figure this out for yourself?"* The older student answers: *"I can tell you what I did, but I don't know whether that will be your path — your family is different and your questions are not exactly my questions. I'll tell you what I tried and what I changed my mind about, and you decide what's useful."* The student is not in a seminar, is not being observed in a formal task, and is not producing identity content about themselves — but they are demonstrating the four observations in a meta-move: engaging with a challenge to their expertise (the peer's implicit question), neither dismissing nor retreating, and actively declining to project their process onto the peer's path. If the observer is present for this exchange (for example, as an overheard corridor conversation or as a passage of a Reflection 360 conversation in which the student reports it), the indicator is met.

*Calibration note — false negative.* The four-observation move does not have to occur in a seminar or formal identity-work setting. It can appear in corridor conversations, peer tutoring, mentoring of younger students, or Reflection 360 reports. What the observer checks for is whether the four clauses of the indicator are evidenced — not whether the setting matches a prototypical identity-work context.

## Observer note — partial evidence at Band F

The Band F indicator stacks four observations in a single clause:

1. Engagement with challenge
2. Absence of dismissal
3. Absence of retreat to a fixed position
4. Absence of projection onto peers navigating different questions

The panel's concern (QA Step 8, Christodoulou) is that stacking four observations in one indicator tends, in practice, to reduce the indicator to whichever observation the observer finds easiest. This note specifies how observers should handle partial evidence — clearly met on some clauses but not all — so that the stacking does not silently collapse into a single-clause proxy.

**Recording partial evidence.**

- If the observer sees clause 1 (engagement) and at least one of clauses 2–4, record in the observation log: *"LT 1.3 Band F indicator — clauses [list which ones] evidenced on [occasion]; clauses [list which ones] not yet evidenced."* The absence of a clause is not the same as evidence of its opposite; it is simply "not yet seen."
- If the observer sees clause 1 and none of clauses 2–4, or if the observer sees clauses 2–4 but not clause 1 (e.g. the student is not engaging with the challenge at all, just politely refusing to dismiss it), record: *"Band F indicator — engagement-only / non-engagement-only evidence; return to watch."* This is not a met observation and should not be logged as one.
- If the observer sees a surface-similar case that fails specifically on clause 4 (projection, as in F-NOT-MET-1), record the failure mode: *"Band F indicator — clauses 1–3 met; clause 4 failed (projection: [specific words or actions observed])."* This distinction matters for feedback to the student and for band-promotion decisions.

**Does partial evidence count toward a Band F assessment claim?**

- **Two or three clauses met, across two or more occasions with the same student.** Not sufficient for a Band F claim. Sufficient to describe the student as demonstrating Band F behaviours *inconsistently* — an accurate and useful observation but not a band-promotion claim.
- **All four clauses met on a single occasion, never observed again.** Not sufficient for a Band F claim. Band F is a T3 dispositional LT; a single occasion is a performance, not a disposition. Recall the cross-cutting observation-protocol recommendation from QA Step 8: *"authenticity inferences should be made from cross-context consistency and longitudinal stability, never from a single occasion."*
- **All four clauses met across two or more occasions, triangulated across at least two observers.** Sufficient to support a Band F assessment claim. This is the standard the indicator was designed for.

**Recording convention.** Observers should use a four-cell recording template (one cell per clause) rather than a single tick-box. This is the lightest-weight structural response to the four-observation stacking, and it surfaces partial evidence as partial rather than forcing the observer into a met/not-met binary.
