## Introduction

This exemplar library is a calibration instrument for teachers and co-observers drawing on LT 4.5 T3 observation criteria when forming band claims about students in Band D (G7–8) and Band F (G11–12). Observation indicators in the criterion bank describe what a behaviour pattern looks like in general terms. This library makes each pattern concrete through worked vignettes — specific, school-agnostic classroom and corridor moments that give an observer a reference case before they observe. The question an exemplar answers is not "does the indicator exist?" but "what does this actually look like with a real student, in a real school moment, on a real occasion?"

This library does not replace the T3 observation protocol (T3_observation_protocol_20260423.md), which sets the minimum evidence standard for any band claim across all six T3 dispositional LTs — at least two independent observations across different contexts, triangulation across at least two observers when the claim bears on band promotion, and at least one counterexample observation per unit of work. Those requirements are the floor under this library. Exemplars show what evidence looks like when it is sufficient; they do not lower the evidential standard.

Each section covers one criterion in the following structure: the criterion reference, followed by two or three vignettes of authentic evidence, one or two vignettes of confusable behaviours (false-positive patterns), one or two vignettes of not-yet-seen absence, and calibration notes identifying the most likely misreads and the key distinguishing signal. Vignettes use "a student" and "the student" — no student names, no named schools, no named curriculum programmes or units. Band D vignettes are written for the G7–8 development range; Band F vignettes are written for G11–12.

## Section 1 — Band D Exemplars (Metal + Light Dragons — G7–8, approx ages 12–13)

### 1.1 — crit_0319 | Band D Component 1: Cross-situation strategy use

**Criterion reference:** real-wellbeing-2026-04_crit_0319 | Band D | Applies regulation strategies in practice across multiple emotional situations

---

#### What authentic observation looks like

**Vignette D1-1-MET-A.** A student whose regulation repertoire includes a visible physical anchoring move — pressing palms flat on a surface and taking a breath — is observed using this or a close variant across three different situation types over a term. The teacher records it during a science group project when a classmate dismisses the student's idea; the student puts their pen down, presses their hands on the desk, waits a moment, then responds without snapping. The same student is observed in a PE lesson walking to the boundary of the playing area briefly after a disputed call, collecting themselves, and returning. In a whole-class discussion in a different subject they receive an unexpected criticism from the teacher and pause visibly before responding. The situations are a small-group peer conflict, a physical activity context, and a teacher-feedback moment — three different types. After each, the student returns to full participation without requiring adult support.

**Vignette D1-1-MET-B.** A student uses different strategies in different situations but draws from the same repertoire in all of them: they write briefly in a notebook when academic frustration builds during an individual task; they ask a teacher for a moment before responding when an adult question catches them off guard in a pastoral conversation; they take a brief exit from a group activity after a peer makes a comment they find unfair, then return and resume. None of these specific situations were role-played or coached. The notebook writing, the request-for-a-moment, and the brief exit-and-return are the student's adapted uses of strategies they have been taught in a general context, deployed without prompting in situations they have not been prepared for. The teacher records all three across a single half-term, across three different lesson and pastoral contexts.

---

#### What confusable behaviour looks like

**Vignette D1-1-CONF-A — strategy rhetoric without practice.** A student engages enthusiastically with wellbeing lessons, can name six regulation strategies, and explains their uses accurately. When the teacher watches for deployment during actual emotional moments — a heated group disagreement in history, an unexpected grade return in maths — no observable regulation move occurs in either case: the student argues, shuts down, or walks away without any use of their stated repertoire. Two days later, in a follow-up reflection, the student says "I use breathing when things get intense." The description is likely sincere; the deployment in the moment is not evidenced. Verbal account of strategies is not evidence for this criterion; observable use across situations is.

**Vignette D1-1-CONF-B — single-context application.** Across the observation window, a student uses regulation strategies effectively and consistently in one context: when competitive tension escalates in PE, they reliably stop, breathe, and re-engage. The teacher has six records of this in the PE setting. However, in drama, in a maths group task, and in a pastoral conversation where a difficult topic arises, no regulation move is observed across any of these occasions — the student reacts without pausing in each case. Single-context proficiency is Band C. The Band D criterion requires multi-situation evidence across situation types that are meaningfully different from each other.

---

#### What "not yet seen" looks like

**Vignette D1-1-ABSENT-A.** Across the observation window, the student's emotional activation episodes consistently produce reactive responses — snapping at peers, withdrawing into silence, or leaving the room without notice — without any observable regulation attempt before the reactive move. Adults provide prompts and support after the fact. No self-initiated strategy use before or during an activation episode has been recorded.

**Vignette D1-1-ABSENT-B.** Regulation strategies appear only during dedicated wellbeing or PSHE lessons when prompted by the teacher. Outside those contexts, no independent use is recorded.

---

#### Calibration notes for observers

The Band D claim for this component turns on multi-situation scope, not quality of execution within a single situation. A student who uses a regulation strategy in one familiar context type is at Band C; the move to Band D requires the same repertoire reaching at least three situation types across the observation window. Count the situation types, not the number of uses within one type. The second calibration point is behavioural visibility: the observer needs to see the student doing the strategy, not only reporting having used it. A student who tells you "I breathed through it" after the fact may or may not have done so; a student the teacher observes pausing, pressing their hands flat, breathing, and returning to engagement has provided observable evidence. Rely on direct observation or on cross-context triangulation with another adult who was present, not on student self-report alone.

### 1.2 — crit_0320 | Band D Component 2: Stress-window help-seeking

**Criterion reference:** real-wellbeing-2026-04_crit_0320 | Band D | Recognises stress-response window limits and seeks support proactively

---

#### What authentic observation looks like

**Vignette D1-2-MET-A.** During a week leading up to a school performance event in which a student has a significant role, the student approaches their form tutor at the start of a Tuesday lesson — before any teacher has raised the subject — and says: "I think this week is going to be a hard one for me. Can we check in at the end of the day?" The tutor agrees. On Thursday the student returns between lessons: "I'm getting close — I think I need five minutes before the afternoon." In both cases the student acts before loss of function is visible to an outside observer. After the brief check-in, the student returns to the afternoon session and participates normally. The proactive timing is the evidence: the student is reporting a state before it becomes visible, not after escalation has already occurred.

**Vignette D1-2-MET-B.** During a group project involving sustained peer collaboration, a student pauses mid-discussion, turns to the teacher, and says: "I need to stop for a minute — I can feel I'm getting to the point where I'll say something I don't mean." The teacher has not prompted this. The student takes three minutes, returns, and completes the session without incident. In the observation record, this is the third time in the term the student has done something equivalent — the other two occasions were in a one-to-one pastoral conversation (the student said "I need to slow down before I answer that") and during an assessment task (the student requested a short break before continuing). All three are different context types. All three are proactive, preceding escalation rather than following it.

---

#### What confusable behaviour looks like

**Vignette D1-2-CONF-A — social complaint without monitoring.** A student frequently expresses "this is too much" or "I can't handle this right now" during challenging tasks. The teacher initially records these as window-awareness signals. However, across the observation window, the verbal expression is never followed by a regulation action or help-seek — the student continues engaging (or passively disengages) without requesting support or deploying a strategy after the verbal output. The verbal complaint is emotional communication, a bid for sympathy or acknowledgement; it does not function as the precursor to a proactive action. The criterion requires the combination: recognition signal followed immediately by a regulation or support-seeking move.

**Vignette D1-2-CONF-B — post-hoc vocabulary use.** A student uses stress-window language fluently in reflection sessions and PSHE discussions — "I was definitely outside my window for that whole afternoon," "I could feel my window getting really narrow." The vocabulary is accurate and specific. But in direct observation during actual activation periods across the same term, no real-time monitoring move is seen: the student escalates without self-reporting or acting, and the adult intervenes after the fact. The vocabulary lives in the reflection register; it has not yet transferred to the in-the-moment monitoring function the criterion requires.

---

#### What "not yet seen" looks like

**Vignette D1-2-ABSENT-A.** Across the observation window, the student's overwhelm periods are consistently identified first by the teacher. All help-seeking is responsive to adult initiation: "I can see you're finding this hard — let's take a moment." No record of the student identifying and acting on window-proximity before the adult has noticed it.

**Vignette D1-2-ABSENT-B.** Window-of-tolerance language has not appeared in in-the-moment contexts. The student can explain the concept accurately when asked during a calm lesson but has not been observed using it as a practical monitoring tool during actual emotional situations.

---

#### Calibration notes for observers

The calibration centre for this component is timing. The help-seeking or regulation move must precede the loss of function — it is the proactive step that the student takes because they have recognised the warning signal. A student who seeks support after escalating has produced a very different observation from a student who seeks support before escalating; both are worth recording, but only the latter is evidence for crit_0320. Watch for the in-the-moment verbal marker ("I think I'm getting close") followed by an action — a request, a brief exit, a strategy deployment — not only as a description offered after the episode is over. The window-of-tolerance vocabulary used in real time (not only in reflection) is a strong signal; the key question is always whether any regulation action followed from it immediately.

---

### 1.3 — crit_0321 | Band D Component 3: Relational repair initiation

**Criterion reference:** real-wellbeing-2026-04_crit_0321 | Band D | Initiates relational repair after conflict without teacher brokering

---

#### What authentic observation looks like

**Vignette D1-3-MET-A.** After an argument in a group science activity in which a student said something dismissive to a classmate, the teacher makes a note to check in with both students later. Before the teacher acts on this, they observe the student approach the classmate during a free period later that day, sit down, and initiate a conversation. The teacher is nearby but has not prompted either student. Afterward, the teacher asks the student briefly what they said; the student replies: "I said I shouldn't have dismissed what they put forward. I also said we could try a different approach to the project." The student named their own behaviour specifically — not "I'm sorry if you felt bad" but "I shouldn't have dismissed what you put forward." No adult brokered this conversation or directed either student toward it.

**Vignette D1-3-MET-B.** A student walks out of a lesson after a tense exchange with the teacher. The teacher is planning to speak with the student before the next lesson. The student arrives early for the following lesson, before other students, and initiates: "I want to talk about walking out — I know I shouldn't have done that and it wasn't fair to you or anyone else in the class." The student did not wait for the teacher to frame the situation. They named their own specific action ("walking out"), named its impact on others specifically, and proposed a reset: "Can we just start today fresh?" The initiation is complete before any adult direction.

---

#### What confusable behaviour looks like

**Vignette D1-3-CONF-A — teacher-brokered apology.** After two students have a disagreement, a teacher approaches both and says: "I'd like you both to take a few minutes and think about your part in what happened, and then speak to each other." The students comply. One student says, in the resulting conversation: "I know I shouldn't have said what I said." The acknowledgement is genuine. But this is not crit_0321 — the repair was brokered by the adult who initiated the conversation, created the structure, and directed both students to it. The student's acknowledgement is appropriate and worth noting in the pastoral record; the initiation is not theirs. Teacher-brokered repair is excellent pastoral practice; it is specifically not the independently-initiated repair this component requires.

**Vignette D1-3-CONF-B — defensive acknowledgement.** A student who, when confronted by a teacher about their role in a conflict, responds: "I'm sorry if they felt upset about it — I was just having a bad day." This functions as a social-repair move while avoiding clear acknowledgement of specific own contribution: "if they felt" makes the impact conditional; "I was having a bad day" locates the cause away from the student's action. Compare with authentic repair language at this band: "I said [specific thing] and that was unfair." The specific acknowledgement of the student's own contribution — not softened, not symmetrised, not made conditional — is the distinguishing signal between genuine repair initiation and performed apology under social pressure.

---

#### What "not yet seen" looks like

**Vignette D1-3-ABSENT-A.** Across the observation window, no student-initiated repair move is recorded. All post-conflict resolutions either involve teacher mediation or are left to resolve through time without any acknowledgement from the student. The student avoids the other party for days after incidents.

**Vignette D1-3-ABSENT-B.** After conflicts, the student's consistent pattern is to externalise: framing the other person as solely responsible, seeking adult validation of their own position. No approach or acknowledgement of own contribution is observed.

---

#### Calibration notes for observers

The two most important signals for this component are timing and specificity. On timing: the student must initiate the repair before any adult has directed them to do so. "Go and say sorry," "I think you should speak to them," "you owe them an apology" — all of these disqualify the subsequent action as student-initiated. The observation record should note whether the teacher, another adult, or any explicit direction from an adult preceded the student's approach. On specificity: authentic repair at Band D names the student's own contribution specifically. The calibration question is whether the student says what they did ("I dismissed your idea," "I walked out," "I said that in front of everyone") rather than offering a conditional or symmetrising form ("if you felt upset," "I know I wasn't great either"). Specificity of self-acknowledgement combined with absence of adult direction before the repair move are the two distinguishing signals together.

### 2.1 — crit_0325 | Band F Component 1: High-stakes scaffold-free management

**Criterion reference:** real-wellbeing-2026-04_crit_0325 | Band F | Manages emotional activation in high-stakes situations without adult scaffolding

---

#### What authentic observation looks like

**Vignette F2-1-MET-A.** A student is sitting national examinations across a three-week period. The teacher, who sees this student in a non-examination subject during the same period, observes them arriving consistently, submitting work on time, and initiating substantive conversations with classmates about the subject — behaviour indistinguishable from a non-examination period. The pastoral lead confirms they have had no contact with the student during the examination weeks; no daily check-ins have been offered or requested. In a brief conversation after the examinations end, the student says: "I had a plan. Every morning I ran before school — I knew that was what I needed to stay level. I kept my evenings for things I actually wanted to do. I knew going in that it would be intense and I'd made decisions in advance about what I was going to do during it." Adult support was available throughout; the student did not need to draw on it as a regulating resource.

**Vignette F2-1-MET-B.** A student is managing a significant and ongoing family difficulty (disclosed to the pastoral lead at the start of the term). Across the term, the student's functioning in academic and social contexts does not decline measurably: attendance is consistent, quality of work is sustained, peer relationships are maintained. In a pastoral conversation the teacher initiates at the end of the term, the student says: "It's been really hard at home. But I needed school to be the place where things were stable. I decided that at the start of term. So I kept it that way." The teacher has not been orchestrating the student's functioning — no daily monitoring, no adult-initiated check-ins during the term. The student has maintained the boundary between the home difficulty and school functioning through self-directed management across a full term.

---

#### What confusable behaviour looks like

**Vignette F2-1-CONF-A — dissociation or emotional numbing.** A student appears consistently unaffected by high-stakes situations. During examination periods they show little visible stress and function normally. The teacher initially interprets this as effective regulation. However, across the broader observation window, the student's emotional responsiveness is notably flat across all contexts — not only the high-stress ones: they show neither visible enthusiasm when something goes well nor visible disappointment when it does not. In a pastoral conversation the student says, when asked how they manage pressure: "I don't really feel stress about exams. I don't really feel much." Effective regulation produces proportionate emotional responses in lower-stakes situations alongside managed responses in high-stakes ones; affect flatness that does not track context suggests suppression or disconnection rather than management. Distinguish through longitudinal observation of the student's emotional responsiveness range across the year, not only their presentation in the high-stakes period.

---

#### What "not yet seen" looks like

**Vignette F2-1-ABSENT-A.** High-stakes periods consistently produce loss of function that requires adult orchestration to maintain: the pastoral lead runs daily check-ins during examination periods, initiates contact when the student is absent, and actively coordinates the student's day-to-day functioning. The student's continued engagement during these periods is dependent on that active adult management.

**Vignette F2-1-ABSENT-B.** The student manages stress by avoiding high-stakes contexts: declining optional performance opportunities, finding reasons to be absent during accumulating-stress periods, withdrawing from commitments when pressure builds.

---

#### Calibration notes for observers

The key observation question for this component is whether adult scaffolding is present in the background. Cross-check with the pastoral team and the student's other teachers before assigning Band F evidence here: is there invisible scaffolding — daily parental involvement, active mentor management, regular therapeutic support being actively drawn on as a regulatory resource — that is not visible in the classroom? The Band F claim is specifically that the student's regulation is self-directed; available adult support that is not being drawn on is compatible with the claim; active adult management of the student's functioning is not. The confirming evidence is the student's post-event account: a student who can describe what they did during the high-stakes period, naming specific self-directed decisions they made in advance, provides convergent evidence that the management was theirs.

### 2.2 — crit_0326 | Band F Component 2: Repair with authority figures

**Criterion reference:** real-wellbeing-2026-04_crit_0326 | Band F | Initiates relational repair including with authority figures

---

#### What authentic observation looks like

**Vignette F2-2-MET-A.** Following a difficult exchange in which a student was visibly dismissive of a teacher's instruction in front of the class, the teacher notes the incident and plans to speak with the student. The following morning, before lessons, the student approaches the teacher and initiates: "I want to talk about yesterday. I was rude to you in front of the class and that wasn't fair — I think I was already wound up about something else and you got the edge of it. I'd like to get back to normal between us." The student asks what the consequence will be only after the repair conversation is complete. No disciplinary process had been announced; no adult had contacted the student. The student named their specific behaviour, located its cause honestly, named the impact on the relationship specifically ("between us" — not only "on the lesson"), and initiated the forward step before any consequence was in view.

**Vignette F2-2-MET-B.** A student requests a private meeting with a subject teacher after an extended period of low-level tension in that teacher's lessons. The teacher has not flagged the tension formally. In the meeting the student says: "I've been difficult in your lessons this term and I know it. I want to understand whether there's a way forward, because I actually want to do well in this subject and I can't do that while things between us are like this." The student's forward step names what they want from the relationship — to do well in the subject, and to have a working relationship with the teacher as the condition for that — not the avoidance of a consequence or the management of how the teacher perceives them. The meeting was requested by the student, not arranged by the teacher or any third party.

---

#### What confusable behaviour looks like

**Vignette F2-2-CONF-A — consequence-driven repair.** A student is called to a meeting with a head of year for persistent disruptive behaviour. Before the head of year speaks, the student says: "I know, I've not been okay lately. I'll do better." The delivery is smooth and the acknowledgement appears genuine. Later in the term a similar pattern appears with a different teacher: after a disciplinary email is sent home, the student is noticeably conciliatory with that teacher the following day. The pattern across observations is consistent — repair follows announced or visible consequence — and no spontaneous repair has been recorded in the absence of a pending consequence. The student has learned that performing repair reduces consequence; this is social competence. The Band F distinction is repair that precedes consequences: the student goes to the teacher before any consequence is known or anticipated, because the relationship matters to them, not because repair reduces the cost of the incident.

---

#### What "not yet seen" looks like

**Vignette F2-2-ABSENT-A.** Across the observation window, no student-initiated repair event with a teacher or other authority figure is recorded. Post-rupture behaviour with teachers is minimal compliance and avoidance; relationships normalise through time without any repair conversation initiated by the student.

**Vignette F2-2-ABSENT-B.** The student's repair capacity appears limited to peer relationships. With teachers, the pattern after rupture is silence, surface compliance, or waiting for the relationship to drift back to normal. No student-initiated approach to an authority figure after a rupture is recorded.

---

#### Calibration notes for observers

This is the most calibration-intensive Band F component. The distinguishing question is not whether the student can produce an acknowledgement — most G11–12 students with adequate social reading can do this — but the relationship between the initiation and any pending consequences. Ask concretely: did the student go to the teacher before any consequence was announced, before any adult contacted them, and before the rupture was named as something requiring resolution? If the repair follows a formal meeting, a parental contact, a teacher naming the rupture, or an administrative consequence, the initiation was prompted rather than self-directed. A second signal is the framing of the forward step: a student repairing because the relationship matters names the relationship in what they want next ("I want things to be okay between us," "I want to be able to do well in your class"); a student repairing to manage a consequence frames the forward step in terms of their future behaviour ("it won't happen again," "I'll be different"). Neither framing is conclusive alone; the two signals read together — timing relative to consequence, and framing of the forward step — make the distinction concrete.

### 2.3 — crit_0327 | Band F Component 3: Re-engagement after significant setback

**Criterion reference:** real-wellbeing-2026-04_crit_0327 | Band F | Re-engages with commitments after significant setback without external initiation

---

#### What authentic observation looks like

**Vignette F2-3-MET-A.** A student fails a significant assessment with implications for their planned next steps. For the following week they attend but contribute minimally — present in lessons, not engaging, eating alone, declining peer outreach. The pastoral lead notes the withdrawal and is prepared to initiate contact. On day eight, the teacher observes the student arriving early to the lesson for the subject in which they failed, setting up materials before others arrive. The teacher asks how they are. The student says: "I decided I had to come back to this. It's not going to go the way I planned but I can't just walk away from it. I sat with it for a week and then I thought: what would I actually lose by not coming back?" No teacher or pastoral lead has contacted the student during the withdrawal period. The re-engagement is with the specific domain of the setback, sustained across the following month.

**Vignette F2-3-MET-B.** A student goes through a significant relationship ending. The withdrawal from social engagement at school is visible to the pastoral lead, who waits to see whether the student will move first. On day eleven, the student appears at the pastoral lead's door and says: "I think I've been gone for a while. I want to come back but I don't know how to start." The pastoral lead supports the planning from this point, but the decision to return and the initiation of the first contact are the student's. Over the following month the student re-engages not only with safe peripheral areas but with the specific social contexts in which they had previously been visible alongside the person the relationship ended with — the domain of the loss, not only adjacent territory.

---

#### What confusable behaviour looks like

**Vignette F2-3-CONF-A — premature surface re-engagement.** A student who, after a significant setback, returns to school the following week. Physical attendance is resumed. The pastoral lead records re-engagement. Over the following month, however, the teacher notices: the student participates minimally in the subject in which the setback occurred; avoids all voluntary involvement in the associated activity; has stopped engaging with the peer group most connected to the setback domain. Physical presence in the building is not the same as re-engagement with the domain. The criterion requires sustained functional return specifically to the area of the setback — not only to peripheral activities or adjacent contexts where the setback is out of view.

---

#### What "not yet seen" looks like

**Vignette F2-3-ABSENT-A.** After a significant setback, the student does not re-engage without adult initiation. Every return has required an adult to make the first contact: an email, a scheduled meeting, a visit from a pastoral lead during a free period.

**Vignette F2-3-ABSENT-B.** The student resumes attendance and some peripheral school activities but does not return to the domain of the setback across the observation window. If the setback was academic, the relevant subject remains unengaged. If it was social, the peer context remains avoided.

---

#### Calibration notes for observers

Two distinctions carry most of the calibration weight here. First, who initiates: excellent pastoral practice may involve a teacher or pastoral lead making first contact after a withdrawal period. That is appropriate care. The Band F criterion requires the student to make the first contact — to decide to return and to act on that decision before an adult initiates re-engagement. Record specifically who moved first in each observed re-engagement. Second, which domain: check whether re-engagement includes the specific area of the setback, not only peripheral aspects of school life. A student who resumes attendance but avoids the subject, activity, or social context of the setback is partially re-engaging. The domain return is the marker, not the building return. Both checks require active observation across a sustained period following the return — brief re-engagement followed by renewed withdrawal does not meet this component.

### 2.4 — crit_0328 | Band F Component 4: Articulated working model

**Criterion reference:** real-wellbeing-2026-04_crit_0328 | Band F | Articulates own emotional pattern as a working model used in real decisions

---

#### What authentic observation looks like

**Vignette F2-4-MET-A.** A teacher, in an unstructured corridor conversation after a lesson, hears a student say: "I nearly said yes to that presentation slot — but Thursday afternoons are when I'm not at my best. I've learned that. I took the Friday slot instead." The conversation has not been framed as reflection and the teacher has not asked about the student's emotional pattern. The student is describing a practical decision, made earlier that day, that used their personal model of their own functioning. When the teacher follows up in a later one-to-one conversation, the student gives a specific account that matches the casual remark: they name Thursday afternoons, name the conditions that produce that (end-of-week accumulation, a specific prior commitment that leaves them depleted), and describe what they consistently do differently as a result. The formal account and the casual reference describe the same model — it is not only produced for the formal conversation.

**Vignette F2-4-MET-B.** At the start of a high-pressure group project, a student messages the teacher: "Group projects during busy periods are where I tend to go wrong — I get too in my head and stop communicating with people. I've already told the group I might need a day offline halfway through, so they're not caught off guard. Is that okay with you?" The student has used their personal model to predict a risk in a future context they have not yet entered, and has designed a pre-emptive strategy based on the prediction. The model is forward-looking and operational: it is generating decisions and communications before the situation arrives, not only describing what happened when it did.

---

#### What confusable behaviour looks like

**Vignette F2-4-CONF-A — wellbeing vocabulary performance.** A student produces consistently thoughtful and specific formal reflection documents across the year: what activates them, what helps, what doesn't work. The quality of the formal reflections is high. However, across the same year, the teacher cannot identify a single example of the student referencing their model in an informal context, in a spontaneous conversation, or in a decision they describe having made outside a reflection task. All evidence for the model is located within the school's formal reflection format and in assessment-adjacent conversations. The model may be genuinely developing; whether it is operational in actual decision-making is unresolved. Absence of cross-context evidence is not evidence of absence of the model — but Band F requires the model to be evidenced in non-assessment contexts. This is evidence that the student can engage articulately with the reflection format; it is not sufficient for a Band F claim on this component.

**Vignette F2-4-CONF-B — generic model without personal specificity.** A student can explain emotional self-management fluently and accurately: "When you're under sustained stress, your window narrows and strategies you normally access become harder to reach. The main things that help are physical regulation and predictable structure." This is accurate. When the teacher asks about the student's own specific pattern, the account stays at the general level: "I tend to get stressed about results. I use exercise." There is no specificity about what conditions produce this student's triggers, which strategies work for this student in particular and which backfire, or what conditions this student has learned to build into their environment. A working model includes personal specificity: these are my specific triggers, this is what helps me that might not help others, this is what I have tried that backfires for me. Generic self-management knowledge is not a working model of an individual's own pattern.

---

#### What "not yet seen" looks like

**Vignette F2-4-ABSENT-A.** The student's account of their pattern is confined to formal reflection moments. No cross-context evidence exists — no informal references, no casual mentions, no described decisions that drew on the model — that the model operates as a working tool outside structured reflection.

**Vignette F2-4-ABSENT-B.** The student's account of their pattern does not include what does not work for them. The account is one-directional: these are the strategies that help. There is no evidence of genuine pattern knowledge: what I have tried that backfires, what I do instinctively that makes things worse, what conditions I need that I don't always have. One-dimensional accounts are characteristic of taught knowledge rather than lived self-knowledge.

---

#### Calibration notes for observers

**Partial-evidence protocol for crit_0328.** This component meets later than the other three Band F components for most students. A student who has evidenced components 1, 2, and 3 but not yet 4 is at a well-established point in the Band F observation window, not stalled. Treat this as normal developmental sequencing: regulation practices consolidate first; the articulated model tends to crystallise after the practices have been running long enough to reveal the student's specific pattern clearly. Per the T3 observation protocol section 4, record which components are met and which are not yet seen; do not hold the entire Band F claim open because component 4 is absent. The partial evidence state for component 4 specifically should note: whether the student's verbal account matches their observed regulation behaviour (convergence between stated model and observed pattern is evidence of a genuine model, not a performed one); whether any cross-context evidence exists (casual references outside formal reflection tasks, practical decisions that drew on the model); and whether the account includes specificity about what does not work (the mark of lived rather than taught self-knowledge).

The key calibration distinction is between a model that is reported and a model that is used. A student who gives a fluent, accurate, and personal account of their emotional pattern in a formal reflection but cannot reference it spontaneously, who does not draw on it in decisions, and whose observed behaviour does not obviously correspond to their stated model, is providing reflection performance rather than working-model evidence. The confirming signal is cross-context appearance: the same pattern named informally in a corridor, used to make a practical decision, or drawn on when reasoning about a future situation the student has not yet encountered.

## Section 3 — Cross-band calibration notes

Two patterns recur across Band D and Band F and require the same observer discipline at both levels.

**Authenticity versus performance of authenticity.** At Band D, the main risk is repair initiation that followed an adult direction the teacher does not recall giving, or a defensive acknowledgement that has the form of repair without the specific self-acknowledgement. At Band F, the risks are repair that follows consequence-awareness (crit_0326) and working-model articulation that is produced only in formal reflection contexts (crit_0328). The underlying discipline is the same across bands: look for evidence in contexts where performance is not invited, and look for timing that precedes adult direction or formal assessment framing. Behaviour that occurs without prompting, before consequence, before structure, and in informal contexts carries more weight than the equivalent behaviour in structured or prompted settings. This does not mean unstructured evidence is always sufficient and structured evidence is always suspect; it means that when evidence exists only in structured contexts, the observer should ask whether the behaviour transfers outside those contexts before making a band claim.

**Student-under-stress versus student presentation.** T3 dispositional evidence is most informative when the student is actually activated. A student who discusses their stress-response window fluently during a calm tutor group conversation is demonstrating curriculum knowledge; the same student who uses the concept in the moment of actual escalation is demonstrating crit_0320. A student who describes their repair approach thoughtfully in a reflection task is demonstrating the articulation capacity; the same student who initiates repair before any adult frames it as needed is demonstrating crit_0321 or crit_0326. Observers should note the emotional context of each observation in their records: observations made during actual activation, setback, or relational rupture carry more evidential weight for T3 dispositional claims than observations made during calm or reflective contexts. The disposition is evidenced most clearly when it is actually needed.
