## LT 4.5 — Emotional Self-Management in Practice

**LT Name:** Emotional Self-Management in Practice
**LT ID:** LT 4.5
**Competency:** 4 — Consent, Safety & Healthy Relationships
**Knowledge Type:** T3 Dispositional. This LT only exists as an enacted pattern across time and multiple contexts; no single task produces adequate evidence; the pattern of behaviour across repeated occasions IS the capability. Reasoning: emotional self-management is not a proposition the student can state or a procedure they can execute on demand — it is a disposition that deepens through co-regulation, practised strategy use, relational experience, and recovery from setback over years. Assessment by observation over a sustained window, not by rubric on a single occasion.
**Do Evidence Type:** Disposition. All Do cells are third-person observation indicators. Assessment is through multi-informant observation over time; no single performance occasion is sufficient.
**Band Scope:** A–F. Band A (K–2, approx ages 5–7) is authored with scaffolded co-regulation as the observable. Developmental research confirms children at this stage consistently accept adult co-regulation and begin to use named strategies alongside adult support — this is assessable through observation. N/A is not warranted at Band A.

**Prerequisites:**
- **LT 4.4 (Emotional Development & Relationships) — conceptual accelerator.** LT 4.4 provides the analytical toolkit (PFC/amygdala model, dual-process processing, stress-response window, habit formation, healthy/unhealthy relational patterns) that, when in place, makes regulation strategies portable to novel emotional situations and deepens the student's understanding of *why* strategies work. Not a logical gate: students can develop genuine emotional self-management through practised strategies and adult co-regulation without the full LT 4.4 toolkit — but LT 4.4 dramatically improves portability to situations the student has not encountered before.
- **LT 1.1 (Self-Awareness & Regulation) — soft enabler throughout.** LT 1.1 is the Competency 1 T3 dispositional LT covering self-awareness and emotional regulation. Recognising emotional states is the developmental precursor to regulating them. LT 4.5 observation evidence is richer when LT 1.1 dispositions are established, but LT 4.5 does not logically depend on LT 1.1 band-by-band: the two LTs develop in parallel and reinforce each other. Do not gate LT 4.5 observation on LT 1.1 band claims.
- **LT 4.2 (Puberty, Health & Safe Choices) — soft enabler at Bands C–D only.** Puberty biology content informs self-management during early adolescence (explaining intensity spikes, hormonal effects on window of tolerance) but does not gate Band E–F dispositional work.

**LT Definition:** I can manage my own emotional activation in practice — noticing it as it arises, using regulation strategies I have been taught, seeking support when I recognise I am overwhelmed, and repairing relationships after conflict — consistently across contexts and with decreasing reliance on adult scaffolding over time.

## KUD Table

### Band A — Water + Air Dragons — K–2 (approx ages 5–7)

**Know:**
1. A regulation strategy is something specific a person can do to help their body calm down when a feeling is strong — for example: slow breathing, moving to a quiet space, or asking a trusted adult.

**Understand:** When a feeling is big, there are things I can do that help — I do not have to wait for the feeling to disappear on its own.

**Do:** The teacher notices the student, when experiencing a strong feeling (anger, fear, distress, or overwhelm), accepting a trusted adult's co-regulation support — such as being guided to breathe, moving to a calm space, or receiving appropriate physical comfort where school and family norms permit — and attempting to use at least one named regulation strategy alongside or following that adult support, across multiple occasions in the observation window.

**Progression lever:** Baseline — co-regulation accepted; named strategy attempted with adult support present. Foundation for all subsequent independence moves.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Zeman, J., Cassano, M., Perry-Parrish, C., & Stegall, S. (2006). Emotion regulation in children and adolescents. *Journal of Developmental & Behavioral Pediatrics*, 27(2), 155–168.

### Band B — Earth Dragons — G3–4 (approx ages 8–10)

**Know:**
1. A regulation strategy works best when used before a feeling becomes overwhelming — noticing the feeling early gives more options than waiting until it is very big.

**Understand:** I can feel a feeling and still choose how to act — the feeling does not have to choose for me.

**Do:** The teacher notices the student, when upset or emotionally activated, using at least one named regulation strategy with adult prompting; and beginning, on some occasions across the observation window, to initiate a regulation strategy independently before the situation escalates — without needing the adult to suggest it first.

**Progression lever:** Independence (A→B): adult-led co-regulation → student-initiated strategy use beginning to emerge without prompting. Precision: responding to a big feeling → noticing the early signal and acting sooner.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Gross, J. J. (2015). Emotion regulation: Current status and future prospects. *Psychological Inquiry*, 26(1), 1–26.

### Band C — Fire Dragons — G5–6 (approx ages 10–12)

**Know:**
1. Automatic reactions — such as snapping, going quiet, or walking away — are fast responses that happen before conscious thinking catches up. A person can practise interrupting an automatic reaction by noticing the feeling before acting on it.

**Understand:** Noticing what I feel before I act gives me a moment of choice — and that moment of choice is where my self-management lives.

**Do:** The teacher notices the student, in familiar emotional situations (peer conflict, disappointment, or frustration during collaborative work), using a named regulation strategy without adult prompting; and across the observation window, on at least one occasion attempting to interrupt an automatic response before acting, or seeking a trusted adult proactively before a situation escalates.

**Progression lever:** Independence (B→C): prompting required → self-initiated in familiar situations. Scope: single-strategy use → beginning to interrupt automatic responses; beginning to seek support proactively before escalation rather than after.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Siegel, D. J., & Bryson, T. P. (2011). *The Whole-Brain Child: 12 Revolutionary Strategies to Nurture Your Child's Developing Mind*. Delacorte Press.

### Band D — Metal + Light Dragons — G7–8 (approx ages 12–13)

**Know:**
1. *Cross-reference LT 4.4 Band D Know item 4 (dual-process models, stress-response window, habit formation).* Applied to practice: the stress-response window concept explains why regulation strategies learned in calm moments are harder to access when emotional activation is already high — this is not failure of willpower but the predictable effect of moving outside the window of tolerance. Knowing the window makes it nameable in the moment.

**Understand:** I can prepare for hard emotional moments before they arrive — if I know what my window of tolerance feels like from the inside and what helps me stay within it, I have a plan I can use when the moment comes rather than searching for a strategy mid-crisis.

**Do:** The teacher notices the student, across multiple emotional situations over the observation window, using taught regulation strategies in practice (not only naming or explaining them); seeking support from a trusted adult when they recognise they are approaching or exceeding their stress-response window; and attempting to repair a relational rupture on at least one occasion — by acknowledging their part in a conflict and proposing a forward step — rather than waiting for the other person to initiate.

**Progression lever:** Complexity (C→D): single-strategy use in familiar situations → multi-strategy navigation of different activation states, structured help-seeking when recognising window limits, first rupture-and-repair initiation. Transfer (C→D): practised strategies in known situations → applying the window-of-tolerance concept to read own emotional state in real time.

*Band D note — conceptual accelerator.* LT 4.4 Band D Know item 4 (dual-process models, stress-response window, habit formation) in place makes this band's Understand portable: students who hold the analytical framework can recognise their window in novel situations they have not been coached through. Proceed without LT 4.4 Band D if not yet established — self-management does not logically depend on the analytical toolkit — but note that portability to unfamiliar situations will be lower.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Siegel, D. J. (1999). *The Developing Mind: How Relationships and the Brain Interact to Shape Who We Are*. Guilford Press.

*Band D note — partial-evidence protocol applies.* Band D Do contains three observable components (see Check A and Authoring Notes). Partial evidence — a student who meets some components but not others — should be recorded specifically per the T3 observation protocol (section 4): which conditions were met, which were not yet seen. "Not yet seen" is distinct from "failed."

### Band E — G9–10

**Know:**
1. *Cross-reference LT 4.4 Band D Know item 4 (habit formation).* Applied to practice: regulation strategies practised consistently across varied contexts become more accessible during high-activation moments — the strategy that feels effortful in early practice becomes a reliable resource over time. Strategies practised in only one type of situation tend not to transfer to different situations.

**Understand:** The strategies that help me most may not be the same across every situation — learning to notice when a strategy is not working and shifting to a different one rather than persisting with it or abandoning self-management entirely is itself a regulation skill.

**Do:** The teacher notices the student, across multiple contexts and situations over the observation window, consistently applying regulation strategies without adult prompting — including in situations that are not practised or familiar (new social groups, unfamiliar stressors, relational conflicts outside usual friendships); recovering from emotional setbacks in ways that do not significantly impair their subsequent functioning; and, on at least one occasion, adapting their regulation approach when a strategy is not working rather than persisting with it or abandoning self-management effort entirely.

**Progression lever:** Scope (D→E): supported, familiar contexts → cross-context including novel and high-stakes situations without adult prompting. Reasoning (D→E): using strategies → monitoring own regulation effectiveness in real time and adapting approach when needed. Independence: adult prompt absent even in unfamiliar situations.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Bonanno, G. A. (2004). Loss, trauma, and human resilience: Have we underestimated the human capacity to thrive after extremely aversive events? *American Psychologist*, 59(1), 20–28.

*Band E note — partial-evidence protocol applies.* Band E Do contains three observable components. Partial evidence should be recorded per the T3 observation protocol (section 4). Progressive consolidation across the observation window is stronger evidence than all three components met on a single occasion.

### Band F — G11–12

**Know:**
1. *Cross-reference LT 4.4 Band F Know item 3 (relational repair — acknowledgement, accountability, and negotiated restoration of trust).* Applied to practice: effective repair following conflict requires the person to first regulate their own emotional activation sufficiently to engage. Acknowledgement and accountability are not consistently accessible while the person is still inside the reactive response — regulation is therefore a prerequisite for repair, not a subsequent separate act. A student who attempts repair while still reactive tends to produce defensive acknowledgement, conditional accountability, or repair that functions as counter-accusation.

**Understand:** My pattern of self-management is built through repeated occasions — some chosen, some shaped by what I have lived through — and unlike a fixed trait, a pattern can be changed if I understand what sustains it. Knowing my own pattern (what activates me, what helps me most, what I tend to avoid and why) gives me the most leverage over it and makes my self-management portable to situations I have not yet encountered.

**Do:** The teacher notices the student, across a sustained observation window and multiple contexts (academic, social, and out-of-school when triangulated), consistently managing their emotional activation in high-stakes or accumulating-stress situations without requiring adult scaffolding; initiating relational repair after conflict rather than waiting for the other person to act first; recovering from significant emotional setbacks in ways that allow re-engagement with their commitments; and, on at least one occasion, accurately articulating their own emotional pattern — what activates them, what helps them most, what tends not to help — as a working model they use to make decisions, not as a performed reflection for an observer.

**Progression lever:** Independence (E→F): adult scaffold absent even in high-stakes or prolonged adversity situations; repair initiated rather than waited for. Reasoning (E→F): using and adapting strategies → metacognitive model of own emotional pattern used as a working tool. Scope (E→F): own self-management in familiar adversity → sustained self-management in accumulating or novel stress; repair as initiating move, not responsive one.

*Band F note — partial-evidence protocol applies.* Band F Do contains four observable components (see Check A and Authoring Notes). Partial evidence — a student who meets some components but not others — should be recorded specifically per the T3 observation protocol (section 4): which conditions were met, which were not yet seen. "Not yet seen" is distinct from "failed." Progressive consolidation across occasions is stronger evidence than all four met on a single occasion.

**Disciplinary warrant:** Gross, J. J., & John, O. P. (2003). Individual differences in two emotion regulation processes: Implications for affect, relationships, and well-being. *Journal of Personality and Social Psychology*, 85(2), 348–362.

## Authoring Notes

### v1 band label error — correction applied in v2

v1 used non-canonical band labels throughout: Metal and Light Dragons were split across Bands D and E (Metal at D, Light at E), "Post-Metal" was used at Band F, and age ranges were treated as the primary identifier (ages 5–7, 7–9, etc.) rather than grade levels. The underlying developmental content authored in v1 was correct — the observables, Understands, and progression levers were scoped for the right developmental territory. The error was labelling only. v2 corrects all labels to the canonical grade-level convention; no KUD content was changed as part of Fix 1.

### Band A decision: co-regulation authored, not N/A

The session brief flagged Band A (K–2) as the most likely N/A candidate. Decision: authored with scaffolded co-regulation as the observable. Rationale: co-regulation is not a lower-bar substitute for self-regulation — it is the developmental precursor through which self-regulation is built. At Band A, consistently accepting co-regulation and attempting a named strategy alongside adult support is the developmentally appropriate form of the disposition, not an absence of it. Marking Band A N/A would remove the dispositional foundation and create an implicit claim that emotional self-management begins at G3–4 (Band B, approx ages 8–10), which contradicts the developmental literature (Zeman et al., 2006) and misaligns with other T3 LTs in the framework (LT 1.1, LT 3.2, LT 5.2 all have Band A content).

### Know layer discipline

All six bands carry exactly one Know item. Bands A–C: original Know items addressing the dispositional territory at each developmental level (regulation strategies as a named category; early intervention in the emotion arc; automatic reactions as interruption candidates). Bands D–F: cross-references to LT 4.4 Know items plus a specific practical-application claim new to LT 4.5. No Know content was invented to fill a cell. No Know content duplicates LT 4.4.

### Relationship to LT 1.1 — boundary clarification

LT 1.1 (Self-Awareness & Regulation, T3) covers the broader self-awareness and regulation capability across Competency 1. LT 4.5 is specifically within Competency 4 (Consent, Safety & Healthy Relationships) and concentrates on emotional self-management as enacted in relational and social contexts — including rupture-and-repair, help-seeking in relational distress, and the deployment of regulation strategies in the specific context of emotional activation within relationships. Overlap is real (both LTs observe regulation in practice) but the framing and context are distinct: LT 1.1 is general; LT 4.5 is specifically relational, tethered to Competency 4's territory of consent, safety, and relationships. Observation evidence may triangulate across both LTs, but criterion-bank entries should be authored separately. Conversation prompts that ask about self-management in relational contexts (conflict with a friend, attraction/rejection, peer pressure) belong to LT 4.5; prompts about self-management in academic or general wellbeing contexts belong to LT 1.1.

### Relationship to LT 4.4 — analytical toolkit as accelerator, not gate

LT 4.4's analytical toolkit (dual-process models, stress-response window, habit formation, healthy/unhealthy patterns) enriches LT 4.5's dispositional performance at Bands D–F by giving students a transferable vocabulary for their own internal experience. The cross-references in LT 4.5 Know items at Bands D–F are explicitly practical-application bridges — showing students *how the concept explains what they experience* in practice, not re-teaching the concept analytically. Teachers who have established LT 4.4 Band D content should activate it when observing LT 4.5 Band D behaviour: a student who says "I knew I was outside my window" is showing LT 4.4 analytical knowledge deployed in LT 4.5 practice.

Teachers who have not yet delivered LT 4.4 Band D content: proceed with LT 4.5 Band D observation. The strategies and help-seeking and repair-initiation are assessable without the named framework. The conversation prompts will look different ("what did you notice in your body?") rather than ("how close were you to your window limit?") but the dispositional evidence is the same.

### Rupture-and-repair as a Band D introduction

The rupture-and-repair move enters at Band D (G7–8), not Band C. This is developmentally grounded: genuine peer relational repair (acknowledging one's own part and proposing a forward step) requires sufficient metacognitive capacity and interpersonal perspective-taking to be assessable, which consolidates during early adolescence. Band C includes proactive help-seeking (seeking a trusted adult before escalation), which is the Band C precursor — the student is not yet initiating peer-level repair but is beginning to seek external support before the situation exceeds their capacity. The criterion-bank brief should specify that Band D rupture-and-repair does not require successful repair (the other party may not respond); it requires the initiation move only.

### Band F pattern-articulation Do — observer-calibration note

Band F Do component (4) — "accurately articulating their own emotional pattern... as a working model they use to make decisions, not as a performed reflection for an observer" — carries the same authenticity-calibration burden as Band F indicators across other T3 LTs in the framework. Per T3_observation_protocol_20260423.md section 1: this is not observable from articulate self-description alone. The Band F claim on component (4) requires evidence that the student's stated model is actually used to make decisions — cross-context evidence (the same model appearing in contexts where performance of self-awareness is not invited) and longitudinal consistency across occasions (not only in Reflection 360 or assessment moments). A student who gives a fluent, articulate account of their emotional pattern in a formal reflection but shows no evidence of using the model in real-time decisions is providing a data point, not Band F evidence for this component.

### Band F Understand revision — agency framing (v2)

The v1 Band F Understand opened "The way I manage my emotions is a pattern I have built through repeated choices." This framing implies that the student's regulation pattern is wholly self-authored through deliberate choice. Panel review (Wineburg) flagged that this pathologises students whose patterns were significantly shaped by adversity, trauma, or structural conditions beyond their control: a student who learned emotional shutdown because expressing emotion was unsafe, or who developed hypervigilance because their environment required it, did not "choose" that pattern in any meaningful sense. The v2 revision — "built through repeated occasions — some chosen, some shaped by what I have lived through" — preserves the generative insight (patterns are changeable; understanding sustaining conditions gives leverage) while removing the overclaimed agency. The transfer value is increased, not reduced: students who have experienced adversity can now hold the Understand without it implying their pattern is their own fault.
